In many organisations, psychological safety is still treated as something extra: a nice conversation about culture, a trust workshop, a poster on the wall. But anyone who designs learning programmes — especially those who use **Play** as a technique — knows that safety is not a side condition. It *is* the work.

Without psychological safety, there is no honest reflection. Without reflection, no experimentation. Without experimentation, no real learning. That chain sits at the heart of much of what L&D professionals worldwide have been discussing lately — and it explains why some training lands while other programmes do not.

## Play requires a magic circle

Johan Huizinga described play as a **magic circle**: a separate space with clear rules, where different behavioural norms apply than outside it. That is precisely why Play works so well for learning. People can make mistakes, explore new roles, and practise choices — as long as the environment allows it.

That circle collapses the moment someone thinks: *If I get this wrong, I will be judged.* Then play stops. Then learning becomes obligation again.

At Preludens, we design MicroGames and narrative scenarios with that circle explicitly in mind. Not to soften difficult topics, but to give people room to **practise** before it truly matters.

## Five moves you can practise today

In [The Learning Brief](https://ldshakers.substack.com/p/playing-for-psychological-safety) — the newsletter from the L&D Shakers community — psychological safety featured prominently. The core idea: do not treat safety as an abstract concept, but as something you **play** — with concrete moves in conversations, workshops, and team rituals.

Here are five of them, translated into what we see working in learning programmes:

### 1. Invite other perspectives

One strong opinion quickly dominates a group. Ask explicitly what is still missing:

> *"We have heard one direction. Who wants to argue the opposite?"*

In a MicroGame scenario, this means designing choices where multiple answers are defensible — and letting participants hear *why* colleagues chose differently.

### 2. Normalise "I don't know yet"

Leaders who only show certainty make asking questions feel risky. The sentence *"I don't have a clear picture of this yet — walk me through how you see it"* gives others permission to do the same.

That is not weakness. It is the basis for **learner behaviour**: making learning visible instead of hiding mistakes.

### 3. Listen to understand, not to reply

The discipline of not forming a counter-argument yet — but first reflecting back what you heard — makes conversations safer:

> *"If I understand you correctly, it is not the deadline itself, but unclear dependencies. Is that right?"*

In workshops, we use this as an explicit step: summarise first, then respond.

### 4. Treat mistakes as learning data

When a team treats a failed attempt as a surprise, nobody learns to take risks. Reframe deliberately:

> *"What did this show us that we did not know before?"*

That aligns seamlessly with Play: in a game, failure is part of the process, not the endpoint. The question is always: *what do you do with what you discovered?*

### 5. Check whether everyone could participate

At the end of a session, ask whether people felt safe enough to be open. A simple 1-to-5 scale often reveals more than a survey form.

> *"Was there something you wanted to say but held back?"*

That question belongs in every GameStorm wrap-up — and in every learning environment serious about behaviour change.

## From playbook to learning experience

The word *play* is meant literally here: not as a gimmick, but as a repeatable **move** you can practise, adapt, and embed in team rituals. Just as a game designer tests mechanics until they work, you can design safety as an L&D professional or team leader — move by move.

That is also why we do not see Play separately from story and data. A good scenario invites choices. Safe conditions mean people make those choices honestly. And interaction data shows where things stick — so you can adjust with purpose.

## What can you do tomorrow?

Pick one move from the list above. Not five at once — one. Try it in your next team meeting, workshop, or learning moment. Notice what changes in who speaks, who stays quiet, and how honest the feedback is.

You do not build psychological safety in a single session. But every time you deliberately make room for experimentation, you strengthen the magic circle where learning can actually happen.
